Three Lanes of Active Work

The Shared Prosperity Model in practice — industry, entrepreneurship, education.

The thesis is not finished. It is being tested in the places where the next economy is actually being built — in major industrial investments landing in specific regions, in founders navigating the transition from operating a business to owning one, and in classrooms where the next generation of citizens is learning to read the economic system they live inside. Each lane is a different surface of the same argument.

I Industry

Workforce, Shared Ownership, and Community Infrastructure

Major industrial investments are reshaping the American economy — semiconductor reshoring, energy infrastructure, healthcare delivery, advanced manufacturing. These are the sectors where capital is moving at scale and where the next decade of American economic development will be largely decided.

Almost none of this investment is being designed, from the start, to produce broad-based community ownership and durable workforce pathways. We work with industry partners, government agencies, and community institutions to change that pattern at the project level — designing the workforce pipelines, ownership pathways, and community engagement architecture that determine whether major industrial investment produces broad-based prosperity or extraction.

Current Engagements

  • Puerto Rico — Semiconductor Reshoring. With AGMI and Invest Puerto Rico on the workforce and community engagement architecture for the island's emerging semiconductor industry. The thesis: getting this design right in Puerto Rico produces a template for every other reshoring site that follows.
  • California & Colorado — Worker-to-Owner Care Economy. Our concept paper Building a New Care Economy develops an enterprise-level model for redesigning care delivery around worker ownership and community benefit. Partnerships to test the model in practice are in formation.
  • Emerging — Cooperative Health Plan Formation. Early-stage conversations on the application of cooperative governance to health insurance delivery, building on the rural electric cooperative model.

II Entrepreneurship

Founder Transitions and Ecosystem Building

Business ownership is hard, and the founders who make it past the early years have demonstrated discipline, business acumen, and personal capacity that most people do not. That part is real. Beyond the execution challenge, there is a structural one — and the structural challenge is the part most founder support programs underweight.

The founders who reach the $2–$5 million revenue threshold and stall are not stalling because they failed at execution. They are stalling because the next stage requires them to transition from a founder-centric business to one with transferable assets, replicable systems, and institutional credibility. We develop the frameworks, the cohort experiences, and the ecosystem infrastructure that make that transition possible at the scale where it actually matters: procurement, contracts, and capital.

Current Engagements

  • National Small Business Association — Founder Owner Cohort. In partnership with the NSBA, the inaugural Founder Owner Cohort launches September 22, 2026 in Washington DC. For founders in the $2M–$5M revenue range working through the structural transition from operator to owner. Curriculum centers on procurement readiness, contract pipelines, and access to capital that finances enterprise growth rather than founder survival.
  • The Book — Never Build Alone. The intellectual foundation for the cohort. The book reframes the founder transition as a relational and structural challenge — the founders who break through stop building alone. Forthcoming.
  • Care Economy Application. The Owner-Operator Apprenticeship pathway from Building a New Care Economy applies the same logic to a sector-specific case — frontline care workers transitioning to licensed Board and Care operators.

III Economic Democracy

Education and Civic Formation

Political agency without economic literacy is fragile. Most Americans are taught how their political system works — the three branches, the role of voting, the structure of constitutional rights — and almost nothing about how their economic system works. By the time most citizens encounter these questions in adult life, they encounter them through partisan media, financial marketing, or personal crisis — none of which is designed to build the kind of structural understanding democracy actually requires.

We work with educators, universities, K–12 networks, and civic engagement institutions to develop the curriculum, frameworks, and partnership pathways that make economic literacy a foundational element of civic formation — not an afterthought.

Current Engagements

  • The Economic Democracy Curriculum. A curriculum framework that teaches young people to think structurally about economic systems — how power and democracy intersect with economy and capitalism, how political agency depends on economic understanding. Grounded in primary sources, structured for late high school and first- and second-year undergraduate audiences. Live at economicdemocracy.co.
  • Model Projects Built for the AI Era. The curriculum's pedagogy is grounded in role play, public demonstrations of learning, and primary source analysis — AI-proof by design, because the assessments require discernment, dialogue, and in-person performance that recall-based curriculum cannot replicate.
  • Institutional Partnership Development. Active conversations with the Hoover Institution's Center for Revitalizing American Institutions (Stanford), the UC Berkeley History Social Science Project, Kellogg School of Management (Northwestern), the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago's civic engagement office, the Noble Schools network, and Jesuit College Preparatory of Dallas through the Ignatian Solidarity Network. The goal is institutional pilots in academic year 2026–2027.
