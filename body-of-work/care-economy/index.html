Building a New Care Economy

A Worker-to-Owner Enterprise-Level Exploration

Alfredo Mathew III · SPCC.1

Abstract
Healthcare is the defining economic and social challenge facing the United States today, shaping family finances, workforce stability, public budgets, and the health and dignity of an aging population.

California's care economy reflects this national reality in amplified form. It faces a structural crisis of affordability, workforce sustainability, and healthspan. Demand for home- and community-based care is rising rapidly, yet the enterprises delivering care remain fragmented, undercapitalized, and structurally misaligned with public financing systems.

This concept paper proposes a disciplined enterprise-level exploration: California's Worker-to-Owner Care Initiative. Rather than adding another workforce program, this effort focuses on strengthening the care delivery layer itself by testing whether redesigned enterprise structures, shared services, and structured worker ownership pathways can improve stability and workforce outcomes within existing public financing constraints.

Grounded in a statewide listening campaign with workers, providers, policymakers, funders, and ownership experts, the initiative explores two complementary pathways: (1) a structured apprenticeship-to-owner-operator model in regulated care settings, and (2) a trust-based worker equity model embedded within a consolidated care enterprise platform.

01 · The Challenge & Opportunity

A Dual Crisis of Affordability and Healthspan

The United States is facing a dual crisis of affordability and healthspan in an aging society. Americans are living longer than ever, yet spending more of those years in poor health. By 2030, one in five Americans will be over the age of 65, and the population aged 85 and older will double shortly thereafter. California alone must add hundreds of thousands of frontline care workers by 2035, yet the prevailing job structure offers limited stability, mobility, or long-term economic security.

At the same time, families now spend more on childcare and eldercare than on housing, while the care workers who sustain the system remain among the lowest-paid service workers in the economy, with annual turnover frequently exceeding 60 percent.

By 2030, one in five Americans will be over the age of 65, and the population aged 85 and older will double shortly thereafter. An aging society will require more care and caregivers.

As demand rises, the care delivery system is fragmenting and destabilizing. Care is provided primarily through thousands of small, community-based operators that lack access to capital, shared services, or succession planning. Public systems are overextended and reactive. Private equity is consolidating care markets because economies of scale make financial sense even when profits are generated through workforce compression, cost-cutting, or reduced continuity of care.

The labor pyramid has inverted: in 1960 there were five working-age adults for every elder; by 2030, that ratio will fall below three-to-one.

California sits at a crossroads and at the center of this national crisis. Nearly 40 percent of adults over 65 live with a disability or chronic illness requiring assistance. These conditions make clear that the care economy is not facing a temporary shortage or a cyclical disruption. It is starkly confronting a structural mismatch between demographic reality, enterprise capacity, and workforce design that existing systems cannot absorb without fundamental changes to how care is financed, delivered, and sustained.

02 · When Wages Are Not Enough

Enterprise Design as a Workforce Strategy

Much of today's health care debate focuses on who pays — patients, employers, insurers, or the government. While those questions matter, they often amount to cost shifting rather than addressing the underlying drivers of rising costs, workforce instability, and uneven outcomes. The deeper issue is how care is financed, incentivized, and organized at the enterprise level.

Public systems increasingly bear the consequences of this misalignment. When care enterprises remain fragmented and undercapitalized, public dollars flow toward crisis response, institutional care, and emergency interventions. Investment is repeatedly absorbed by turnover, compliance burden, and short-term survival rather than long-term stability.

In this environment, wage increases alone cannot resolve instability. Wages are constrained by enterprise economics. Training programs can improve entry into the field, but they cannot fix fragile business models. If the enterprises beneath the workforce remain unstable, workforce gains will not compound.

The opportunity is not to train more workers into a fragile system. It is to intentionally redesign the enterprises and financing structures that shape how care is delivered and sustained.

03 · What Phase I Revealed

Key Insights from 20 Interviews

Phase I consisted of a focused listening campaign with 20 stakeholders across California's care ecosystem. Taken together, the findings validate a central conclusion: the care system's strain is not primarily a labor shortage or a funding gap — it is a problem of enterprise and incentive design within constrained public financing.

Insight 01

Scarcity Is Structural

There is no new pool of money coming for elder care. The challenge is not how to eliminate subsidies, but how to deploy existing dollars more effectively.

Insight 02

Enterprise Fragility Drives Workforce Fragility

Workforce instability is downstream of enterprise instability. Training and wage increases layered onto fragile enterprises do not compound; they dissipate through turnover and operational strain.

Insight 03

Demand Is Not the Problem — Design Is

Demand for care is high and growing. Yet dollars and workers fail to translate into stable capacity because enterprises lack scale, shared services, and coordinated infrastructure.

Insight 04

Workers Want Advancement and a Stake in Enterprise Success

Workers are open to profit-sharing or equity participation when responsibility is aligned with experience and support — not isolation or undue financial risk.

Insight 05

No Single Model Is Sufficient

Cooperatives, private equity roll-ups, ESOP transitions, and trust-based structures each offer partial solutions. Stakeholders repeatedly emphasized the need to integrate the strongest elements of each within real-world financing constraints.

04 · A Worker's Pathway

What We Are Building Toward

The first part of Shryrah's story reflects her lived experience, shared during the Phase I listening campaign. The second part is illustrative — showing how her trajectory could evolve under the Worker-to-Owner Care Enterprise-Level Demonstration this concept paper proposes.

The Current Reality

Before entering the care economy, Shryrah worked in retail banking. When COVID hit, her branch closed and she was laid off. Soon after, a fire destroyed her housing, forcing an unexpected relocation just as she was caring for a terminally ill parent. She made the difficult decision to draw down her 401(k) to stay afloat — sacrificing long-term security to meet immediate needs.

She enrolled debt-free in a credentialing program designed for working learners. Within months, she secured a frontline caregiver role. Income returned. Confidence followed. The workforce system worked as intended: training led to employment.

But once employed, the pathway forward narrowed. There was no clear way to translate her skills into greater responsibility, leadership, or long-term economic security without undue personal financial risk. The workforce pathway ended at placement.

An Illustrative Future State

Building on the credentialing foundation, we are exploring two structured pathways for workers like Shryrah to participate more fully in enterprise performance.

A supported apprenticeship pathway toward licensed owner-operator roles within regulated care settings; and a trust-based equity pathway that enables frontline workers to participate in the performance of a scaled care platform without assuming individual operational liability.

Advancement would follow defined roles and responsibilities rather than requiring personal capital or independent entrepreneurship. Those who remain in frontline roles would be eligible to participate in profit-sharing through an employee trust. Mobility and participation are tied to enterprise performance and tenure — not personal debt or balance-sheet exposure.

05 · An Integrated Approach

The Shared Prosperity Care Enterprise Model

The Shared Prosperity Care Enterprise Model addresses the central constraint identified in Phase I: workforce investments cannot deliver durable outcomes when the enterprises employing workers remain fragile, fragmented, and under-resourced. The model introduces a self-reinforcing operating system that strengthens care delivery enterprises so workforce advancement, care quality, and public investment compound over time rather than dissipate.

At its core, the model functions as a flywheel composed of four coordinated components. Each reinforces the others; none is sufficient on its own.

Together, these core components form a disciplined system that aligns economic performance with public benefit. Enterprise aggregation creates stability, shared services enable scale, and trust-based participation allows workers to build long-term security alongside wages.

06 · Two Pathways Under Exploration

Worker-to-Owner in Practice

Phase I made clear that no ownership model can succeed unless the underlying enterprises are financially viable. Worker participation must be built on businesses capable of operating at scale, contracting credibly with public systems, and sustaining themselves without ongoing subsidy.

Pathway 1

Owner-Operator Apprenticeship — Board & Care Model

In regulated settings such as Board and Care homes, ownership can be structured at the individual enterprise level — but only if the business performs at an enterprise standard.

  • High-performing credentialed care workers enter a structured apprenticeship toward licensed owner-operator roles
  • Operational training: compliance, staffing, budgeting, and regulatory navigation
  • Capital deployed to acquire real estate and operating assets
  • Shared services reduce administrative burden and increase stability
  • Revenue mix: Medi-Cal reimbursement, behavioral health funding, selective private-pay

Structured like a franchise model — performance-oriented, not isolated entrepreneurship.

Pathway 2

Trust-Based Worker Equity — Home-Based Care Platform Model

In fragmented home-based care markets, scale is often necessary to achieve enterprise stability. This model aggregates providers into a professionally managed platform.

  • Providers aggregated into a professionally managed platform
  • Shared services — HR, billing, compliance, purchasing, data systems
  • Holding company deploys patient private capital for integration and growth
  • Employee trust owns a defined percentage of the holding company
  • Workers participate in upside without assuming operational liability

Enterprise performance, not subsidy, funds worker participation.

Why This Matters

Balancing worker advancement with enterprise viability requires disciplined design. Ownership participation cannot replace margin; it must be supported by it. The purpose of this exploration is to determine whether care enterprises can meet enterprise-level performance standards while embedding worker participation in their capital structure.

07 · Phase II

Enterprise Discovery and Structured Exploration

The next phase of this work is not an acquisition or pilot launch. It is a disciplined enterprise discovery effort designed to determine whether a Worker-to-Owner model can operate responsibly within California's care economy. Phase II is a 24-month structured exploration designed to move at the speed of both institutions and markets.

Act I

Seed and Structure

Months 0–9

  • Secure initial seed funding for enterprise modeling and due diligence
  • Convene labor, operators, funders, and policy leaders
  • Narrow vertical and geographic focus
  • Develop preliminary financial models and revenue scenarios

This stage cannot proceed without catalytic funding. Philanthropic dollars de-risk analysis and enable credible modeling.

Act II

Validate and Design

Months 9–18

  • Conduct market-level enterprise underwriting
  • Test apprenticeship and trust-based participation structures
  • Model contracting scenarios with counties and managed care organizations
  • Refine governance and capital structure options
Act III

Decide and Structure

Months 18–24

  • Determine whether enterprise-level viability has been demonstrated
  • Align capital sources — public, private, philanthropic
  • Evaluate public value and affordability implications
  • Define guardrails and performance metrics
  • Decide whether to advance to a bounded demonstration

If validated, Phase II will culminate in a responsibly structured demonstration aligned with workforce advancement, enterprise performance, and public financing.

About This Work

Authorship & Intellectual Lineage

This concept paper was authored by Alfredo Mathew III and SPCC.1 — Shared Prosperity Community Corporation. It builds on more than two decades of Alfredo's work in entrepreneurial ecosystem building, ownership design, and the Shared Prosperity Model: a framework for converting fragmented community-based enterprises into structured, equitable, and scalable vehicles for worker ownership and long-term economic mobility.

The care economy application developed here draws directly from that intellectual lineage — applying the same architecture used across workforce, entrepreneurship, and community development contexts to the structural crisis in California's care delivery system. The statewide listening campaign — 20 interviews with workers, providers, policymakers, funders, and ownership experts — was designed, conducted, and synthesized by the SPCC.1 team.

Toby Ewing served as Senior Policy and Systems Advisor, contributing deep expertise in public finance, California's behavioral health infrastructure, and the regulatory and policy sequencing necessary to translate enterprise models into viable public system alignment.

An Invitation to Join Us
The opportunity is not to solve the care economy in one step.

What is required are credible, collaborative efforts that strengthen care delivery at the enterprise level, align workers, providers, payers, and capital around shared outcomes, and improve how public dollars function within real-world financing constraints.

This exploration invites health systems, labor partners, policymakers, philanthropic institutions, and capital providers to participate in a disciplined process of testing what is viable — and what is not.
